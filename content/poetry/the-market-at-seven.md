At seven the market was already old.
The women had been there since five,
arranging tomatoes by the rule of color,
red at the front, the soft ones behind,
the ones that would not last
sold first and fast.

My grandmother moved through it
like water through a crowd —
without parting anything,
arriving somewhere
no one expected.

She spoke a price once.
She never said it twice.
The merchants knew this
and adjusted themselves accordingly.

This was a kind of power
I did not have a name for then.
I understand it now
as the power of people
who have been poor long enough
to know exactly what things cost —

not just in naira,
but in the hour it takes
to make a thing,
in the back bent over a stove
before the sun had opinions,
in the child left sleeping
so the market could be reached
before the best produce disappeared.

There are economies
that textbooks do not describe:
the one that lives in the grip of a woman
who has carried a load on her head
so long her spine has learned
a different language.

At seven the market was already old.
By noon it would be finished.
By one she would be home,
and the stove would be going,
and the smell of stock fish
and palm oil would write themselves
into every room,
and I would learn, again,
that everything we eat
was once carried by someone.
