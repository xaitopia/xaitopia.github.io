The night didn't just pass;

it sat on my chest like a stone for a thousand years.

I counted the minutes in the way a prisoner

counts the cracks in the ceiling —

waiting for a morning I had stopped believing in,

shivering in a room that felt like it was

made of nothing but shadow and cold teeth.

 

Then, a bruise appeared on the horizon.

 

A thin, violet ache that bled into navy,

then a smear of orange — ugly and beautiful —

tearing a seam through the hem of the world.

The stars didn't go out;

they were simply swallowed by a louder light.

 

I stood at the window,

my hands pressed against the glass,

and watched the first true beam of yellow

shatter the gloom of the driveway, the trees, my life.

It hit my face like a physical hand —

warm, unapologetic, and real.

 

The light doesn't ask if you're ready for it.

It doesn't ask if you deserved to make it through the dark.

It just arrives.

 

I took a breath — a real one,

deep enough to hurt —

and felt the heat soak into my collarbone.

The long night is a ghost now.

The world is on fire,

and I am still here to see it burn.
