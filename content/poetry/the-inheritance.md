I was washing rice.

The specific action of it:

hands in the bowl, moving in circles,

the water going cloudy and then clear.

I looked down.

And there they were.

His hands.

Not like his hands. His hands.

The same knuckles, slightly raised.

The same particular width of palm.

The same way the thumb sits,

slightly apart from the rest,

as if it has its own opinions.

Baba died when I was twelve.

I did not know I had been carrying him.

I did not know the body keeps

what the memory sometimes misplaces.

He farmed with these hands.

He built things with these hands.

He held my face in these hands once, on a visit,

the way grandparents hold a child's face —

as if checking that you are still real,

as if they cannot believe you exist,

as if you are the best thing that has happened

in a life that has had many things happen.

I looked at my hands a long time.

The water cleared.

I said, quietly, to no one:

there you are.

I have been looking for you.
