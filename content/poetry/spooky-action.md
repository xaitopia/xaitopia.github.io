The physicists have a word for it.

They call it entanglement.

 

Two particles, once connected,

remain so across any distance —

across the room, across the universe,

it does not matter.

Measure the spin of one

and the other one, instantly, knows.

 

Not quickly.

Not in the time it takes for light to travel.

Instantly.

Faster than anything that has a speed.

 

Einstein called it spooky action at a distance,

which is the most human thing

a physicist has ever said —

because yes.

That is exactly what it is.

That is exactly what you are.

 

When they told you the results on a Tuesday morning

I was in a meeting three cities away

and something went wrong with my hands.

They couldn't hold the pen right.

I didn't know yet.

But I knew.

 

Spooky action at a distance.

 

The physicists will tell you

the particles don't send a signal.

There is no mechanism.

There is no wire between them.

They are simply —

what is the word —

correlated.

Permanently.

In a way that cannot be explained

by anything that happened after they separated.

 

Only by the moment they were one.

 

I think about that sometimes.

How there must have been a moment —

before words, before faces,

before whatever city and whatever year

we each arrived in —

a moment when we were adjacent,

when something in the structure of us

decided: these two.

These two will know each other across rooms,

across silences,

across the bad years and the inexplicable distances.

 

They will feel it

before they are told.

They will know before knowing is possible.

 

The physicists are still arguing about why.

 

I am not.
