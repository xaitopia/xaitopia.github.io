I put on Fela.

I do not know why I always start with Fela,

but the room changes when I do.

Something shifts in the corners.

The air becomes more inhabited.

They come, I think, when the music is right —

not summoned, not commanded,

just drawn the way all things are drawn

to the frequency of joy.

My grandfather's father, who I know only from a single photograph,

one stern face above a collar —

he is not stern now.

Now he moves the way old men move

when they think no young person is watching:

with everything,

with all the years,

the whole long body of a life

that danced through harder things than this.

The women take more space than they ever did alive.

No permission asked now.

No small chair pushed to the edge.

They take the centre of the room

and they do not apologise

and the room is glad of it.

I sit and watch.

I am not afraid.

I am grateful.

This is what the music is for —

not entertainment,

not escape.

This.

The living and the dead

in the same room,

moving together,

briefly,

before the record ends.
