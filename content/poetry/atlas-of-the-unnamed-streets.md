There are streets in this city with no names
on any map I have seen.
You find them by memory —
third junction after the fallen mango tree,
left at the compound where they make suya
until eleven, left again
where the road goes soft with puddles
that never fully dry.

My mother knew every corner of Amichi
the way a palm knows its own lines.
She did not need street names.
She needed the smell of akara frying,
the particular slant of afternoon light
through the neem trees by the water board,
the woman who sold pineapples
and gave her children's names
to all the children passing.

There is a kind of knowledge
that lives in the body before it enters the brain —
the way a foot finds the loose tile
on a staircase it has climbed a thousand times,
correcting without thinking.

I have lived in places with names for everything.
Avenue this. Boulevard that.
Streets that announce themselves
in block letters at every corner,
and still I got lost.

The unnamed streets knew me
better than I knew them.
They held my wrong turns gently,
let me wander back
to the suya smoke and the soft road
and the woman who called all the children
by someone else's name,
and they did not mind.
