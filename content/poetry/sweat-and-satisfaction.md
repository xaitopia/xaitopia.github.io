There is a specific kind of liberation that lives in the bottom of a five-gallon bucket. The wall has been the same shade of "expired-milk-white" for three years — a flat, tired skin that soaked up every bad Tuesday, every midnight doubt, and the scuff marks of a life lived in a defensive crouch. But today, the sound of the metal lid popping is a sharp, metallic note of revolution.

I dip the roller into a tray of Saffron-Summer, and the first stroke is a wet, thick sacrilege against the boredom of the past. There is no grace in the beginning; there is only the rhythmic thwack-schloop of the foam and the smell of chemicals and possibilities. I watch the vibrant gold swallow the gray, burying the ghosts of old arguments and the coffee stains of a version of me I no longer wish to be.

By the second coat, my shoulders ache in that honest, human way that says: I am building this. The light from the window hits the wet surface and bounces back, turning the air in the room into a warm, amber liquid. The floor is a battlefield of drop cloths and splattered tape, and there's a stray dot of yellow on my forehead that I won't wash off for hours.

When the sun finally dips, I don't see a room anymore — I see a dawn. The wall isn't just a boundary; it's a mirror of the pulse behind my ribs. It's loud, it's bright, and it's finally, finally dry.
