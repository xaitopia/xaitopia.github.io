The rope is frayed, a simple bit of twine braided by hands that have seen too much, yet today it feels like a tether to the sun. Your fingers find it, trembling — not from the cold of the infusion room or the ghost-ache in your bones, but from the sheer, terrifying gravity of enough.

One pull.

The brass wakes up with a sudden, golden shout, a clean riot of sound that tears through the hallway. It isn't a melody; it's a declaration. It's the sound of white flags being burned, of every "what-if" drowning in a sea of Right Now.

The nurses stop mid-stride, their eyes crinkling above blue masks — a silent, salt-streaked choir. They've heard this bell a thousand times, but they know that for you, it is the first time the world has ever truly been loud.

Let it ring. Let the vibration sink into your marrow, filling the hollow spaces where the fear used to sleep.

You aren't a chart anymore. You aren't a dosage or a ticking clock. You are the echo vibrating in the air, walking toward the automatic doors while the metal is still warm, still singing your name to the empty rooms.
