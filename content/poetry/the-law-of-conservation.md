In the beginning — not of time,

but of this particular grief —

a woman stood at a window

with one hand pressed against the glass,

and the cold came through.

 

She had been crying for so long

that the tears no longer tasted of salt

but of something older —

copper, maybe, or the inside of a word

you have said so many times

it has emptied out and become only sound.

 

The last tear fell.

 

And here is the part no one tells you:

grief does not disappear.

It transforms.

That is the law — the oldest one,

older than gravity,

older than the naming of things —

nothing is destroyed.

It only changes form.

 

The tear rose.

(You did not see it. You were looking at the floor.)

It rose the way only the truest things rise —

without fanfare, without announcement —

until it reached the place where the atmosphere thins

and the cold is no longer the cold you know,

and it burst open

the way a held breath finally releases,

the way a door opens in a house you thought was sealed.

 

And now there is a new star.

Faint.

You would need to know exactly where to look.

But it is there,

and it is burning,

and it burns with everything you felt

that you did not know how to say,

and it will burn

for longer than the name of any grief

you will ever carry.

 

Look up tonight.

If you see something faint and new

in a part of the sky you have never noticed —

that is yours.

That is where it went.
