He wound it every Sunday without fail,

the way another man might say his prayers —

two turns, no more, the spring held just enough

to carry through the week without complaint.

I never saw him check another clock.

This watch was time. All other clocks were guesses.

It passed to me the morning of the burial

in a small brown envelope, still ticking.

I held it to my ear the way you'd hold

a shell up to the sea — to hear what's carried

inside a thing long after it has left

the place it learned its sound.

And it was ticking.

Not metaphorically. Not in memory.

Actually, physically, ticking —

the same small heartbeat, the same measured insistence

that time continues, that time has an obligation,

that whatever else has stopped, this has not.

I wind it now on Sundays.

Two turns, no more.

The spring holds just enough.

I do not check another clock.
