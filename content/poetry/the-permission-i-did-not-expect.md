It was nothing.

That is what makes it strange.

Nothing significant.

Something a friend said —

a small, stupid, harmless thing —

and before you could stop it,

before you even knew it was coming,

the laugh was already out.

Real. Loud. Yours.

And then the silence after it.

You looked around the room

as if someone had walked in on you

doing something shameful.

As if joy were a betrayal.

As if the dead kept score.

But here is what no one told you:

they don't.

The dead do not need your ruin as proof.

Your grief was never a monument

they required you to maintain.

The laugh meant nothing was forgotten.

It meant the body, in its ancient wisdom,

had decided that you were still alive

and that being still alive

required, occasionally,

the involuntary return of joy.

You are allowed this.

You were always allowed this.

Grief does not have a dress code.

You can be heartbroken

and also,

on a Tuesday,

at something stupid a friend said,

briefly, genuinely,

laugh.
