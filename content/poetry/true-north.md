The compass had been spinning for so long

it had forgotten that spinning wasn't the same as searching.

It had been confusing motion for progress,

the way you can walk a circle for an hour and call it a journey.

 

It was the North Star who spoke first.

 

"You are not lost," she said. "You are disoriented.

Those are not the same thing."

 

The compass kept spinning. "I can't tell which way is north."

 

"I know. That's why I'm here. I haven't moved."

 

This was the hardest thing for the compass to believe —

that something could simply stay,

without flinching,

without being blown sideways by every passing wind.

It had been blown sideways so many times

it had begun to think that steadiness was a myth,

a story told to instruments in better climates.

 

"What do you do," the compass asked,

"when the clouds cover you?"

 

"I wait," said the North Star.

"I am still north whether you can see me or not.

I am still north when the weather closes in.

I am still north when you doubt me.

My being north is not conditional

on your ability to find me."

 

The compass slowed.

Then slowed some more.

 

"How do I get back to myself?" it asked.

 

"Stop moving for a moment," said the North Star.

"You'll feel me. You were built to feel me.

That is the whole of what you are —

a thing made to be oriented

by something that doesn't move."

 

The clouds thinned. A single point of light.

 

The needle stopped its spinning.

Found true north.

Held.

 

"I was always there," said the North Star.

"The question was never whether I existed.

The question was only whether you were still enough

to feel me."
