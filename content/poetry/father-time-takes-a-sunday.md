He has been at it since before the first rooster.

Before rooster even meant rooster.

He has been carrying his scythe on one shoulder

and his hourglass on the other

and frankly,

that sand is heavy.

 

Today — this particular Sunday,

in the gold of late afternoon —

he decided: enough.

 

He found a hammock strung between two oaks

he'd been watching grow since they were acorns

and he lay down.

 

His scythe leaned against a fence post.

His hourglass sat in the grass,

and here is what no one tells you:

when Father Time lies down,

the sand stops.

 

Not forever. Just for a little while.

Just for this afternoon, where the light

comes in at that specific angle

that makes even shadows look warm.

 

He heard a dog bark, somewhere far away.

He heard someone mowing a lawn — then stopping —

then not starting again.

He heard the particular silence of a Sunday

which is different from all other silences:

denser, slower,

full of something that feels almost like permission.

 

He slept.

 

And somewhere, everywhere, people felt it —

that loosening,

that sense that the afternoon

had stretched a little past itself.

That there was more of it today.

That the light was staying

just a few minutes longer than it should.

 

They didn't know why.

 

They just poured themselves another cup,

stayed seated a little longer than they meant to,

and thought:

well.

There's time.
