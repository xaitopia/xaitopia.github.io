O small bright vessel, red against the grey,

you hold the folded note I pressed and creased —

the words my mouth has struggled to convey

since you were taken, since the noise has ceased.

 

I chose the string with care. Tied it with both

my hands the way you used to tie my shoes.

I pressed the crayon hard and kept the oath

and wrote the things I never got to choose.

 

Now go.

Climb past the rooftops, past the church spire,

past where the pigeons make their afternoon noise.

Climb past the places where I used to search

your face for signs that everything was fine.

 

I do not know if heaven has a mailbox,

or if the clouds open for paper and ink.

But I believe in the lifting — in the float —

in how the string went slack and I let go.

 

That is the whole of it, I think:

the going slack. The letting go.

The watching something leave your hand

and choosing — just this once — to not

run after it.

 

O small red globe, now smaller, now a speck,

now gone — please find them.

Tell them what I wrote.

Tell them the crayon was their favourite colour.

Tell them I am still here,

and still, some Sundays,

I look up.
