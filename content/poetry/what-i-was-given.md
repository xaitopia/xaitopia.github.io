From her I got the laugh — the room-filling kind,

that comes before the joke has reached its end,

that bends the body forward, and the mind

surrenders to it wholly, without pretend.

From him I got the stillness when the storm

arrives at the front door and will not leave —

the jaw that sets, the hands that remain warm,

the eyes that say: we will not fall. Believe.

I used to see them as opposing forces,

the loud and open, the quiet and contained.

But now I understand the deeper sources:

both knew what it cost to have remained.

She laughed because she chose to face the light.

He held because he would not yield the night.
