We did not move it.

That is the first thing you should know.

We did not move it because no one said to,

and no one said to because no one could.

It is still at the table at the angle you always left it —

slightly pulled out,

the way a person leaves a chair

when they mean to come back.

We eat around it now.

We pass the jollof rice past it,

the fried plantain,

the pot of egusi that takes all afternoon to make

the way you made it,

the way none of us have made it right since.

Sometimes someone puts their bag on it.

Then someone else quietly moves the bag.

Nobody says anything.

We all know.

There are Sundays when the light falls on it

at exactly the angle it fell on you —

and for a moment the room does the thing

that rooms do after a loss:

it holds the shape of the person

a little longer than physics requires.

We notice. We do not speak.

We pass the food.

We eat together.

We leave the chair.
