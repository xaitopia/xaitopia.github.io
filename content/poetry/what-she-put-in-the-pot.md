There is no written recipe. There has never been a written recipe. The knowledge lives in the hands.

Great-great-grandmother learned it in the years before independence, in a kitchen that was also a prayer room, stirring a pot over firewood while outside, the world was being decided by people who did not consult her. She made the soup anyway. She made it with everything she had.

It passed down. Each woman adding what her season gave: the grandmother who added extra crayfish because that was the year of abundance; the one who reduced the pepper because she was feeding children who had survived something; the one who made it every day for three months after her husband died, because the body still needs feeding even when the heart has gone on strike.

My mother makes it on Sundays. I have watched her a hundred times. I have never once successfully replicated it.

She says: it's the same recipe.

But she says it with her hands moving, and her hands know things she has not translated into words, and I suspect she does not know she knows them.

This is how the women of my line have always passed their wisdom:

not in writing,

not in instruction,

but in doing,

with you beside them,

learning without knowing you are learning,

carrying without knowing what you carry —

until one day your hands move on their own,

and the pot smells right,

and you understand that you were taught

every time you were simply present.
