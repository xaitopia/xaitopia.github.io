O blessed bell, O distant, tinny promise,

you were the best news any afternoon could bring.

Three streets away and we were already running —

not toward you exactly,

but toward the version of ourselves

who had not yet been disappointed by the world.

In our neighbourhoods you came on a bicycle.

You did not have a truck. You did not have a song.

You had a bell and a white cooler

and a kind of unhurried confidence

that told us you knew exactly what you were carrying

and what it meant to us.

Fan Ice. Iced Lolly.

The particular cold of a thing that has been packed in ice

since before the sun got serious.

We ran to our mothers.

We asked in the tone of people to whom this mattered more

than anything had mattered before

or would matter for at least another hour.

Sometimes: yes.

Sometimes: no, it is not time yet, who bought you the last one.

But on the yes days —

on the yes days the ice hit the tongue

and the afternoon became a different kind of afternoon,

and the sun was still hot

and the dust was still dust

but we were holding something cold

and it was ours

and it was sweet

and we were running.
