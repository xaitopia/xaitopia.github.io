O, heavy weight of hide and stitched-bound bone,

You smell of ancient dust and tannery,

A silent brick of vellum, tucked and prone,

The keeper of a life's epiphany.

I trace the grain — the skin of something passed —

And feel the pulse of everything to come,

A vessel built to hold and made to last,

While in your presence, I am struck and dumb.

 

I crack the spine; a sharp and woody snap,

A tiny lightning bolt within the room.

The first page lies like snow upon a map,

Or like a ghost emerging from a tomb.

It is too clean. It is a blinding field,

A perfect, cream-colored and vacant stare.

It asks for every truth I've kept concealed,

Yet warns me: "Don't you dare. No, don't you dare."

 

But oh, the ink is heavy in the nib,

A dark and restless nectar wanting out.

To break the silence under every rib,

And carve a name upon this sea of doubt.

I'll mar the white. I'll make the first rough mark,

And let the ink-blood bloom across the page;

A lantern lit against the looming dark,

The turning of the key, the open cage.
