There is a particular music in a full room.

It is the hiss of steam and the knock of the tamper and three separate conversations happening at different volumes and urgencies — one about a broken heart, one about a quarterly report, one about nothing at all, which is the best kind.

Someone laughs. The laugh arrives before you see them. It crosses three tables and becomes part of the room's character. You do not know this person. It does not matter. Their joy is briefly, uselessly yours.

I have sat in spots like this across different cities — in Lagos cafés off Victoria Island where the generator hum becomes its own bass note; in quiet corners of Nairobi and Accra where the ceiling fans move the heat around without reducing it; in rooms where six languages were being spoken simultaneously and somehow it all resolved into one sound, which was: people, living, talking, continuing.

This is what I come for when I am lonely.

Not conversation. Not connection. Not to be noticed.

Just to be inside the noise.

To sit at the edge of other people's aliveness

and feel, by proximity, a little more alive.

The espresso machine hisses.

Someone argues gently.

Someone else laughs again.

The room continues its beautiful noise.

I order another coffee.

I stay a little longer than I need to.

I am not lonely anymore.
