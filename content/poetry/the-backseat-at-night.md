The safest I have ever been in my life was in the backseat of my father's car on the road from somewhere we had been to home.

Night outside. The highway unreeling. Lagos-Ibadan, or the long road east — the kind of drive that takes hours, the kind of dark that has no streetlights, only the car's beam cutting forward and the stars overhead doing what stars do, which is everything and nothing all at once.

In the front seat: my parents. Their voices low, talking the way adults talk when they think the children are asleep — about money, or relatives, or something that had happened that needed discussing. Their voices were not for me. They were not performing anything. They were just two people in the dark, talking through the night, and I was held by the whole arrangement of it.

The engine. The road. The voices. The dark outside.

I was not old enough to carry anything. Nothing was required of me except to be present, to exist, to be one of the reasons the car was going home.

I have spent most of my adult life trying to find that feeling again.

I have not yet found it.

But I know what it was now, which is something:

it was the feeling of being completely contained.

Held on all sides.

Everything necessary already in the car.

The world outside moving at speed but unable to reach you.

Entirely, for once, enough.
