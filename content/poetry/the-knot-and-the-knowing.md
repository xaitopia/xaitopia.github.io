You should have put it away properly.

You know this. The necklace knows this.

The necklace is, in fact, making a point of it.

It has become a sentence you cannot read —

gold looped into impossible grammar,

each attempt to undo it

somehow tightening the argument.

You set it down. You walk away. You come back.

This is the first lesson:

some knots only yield to the patient hands,

to the returning.

You find a pin. A thumbnail.

You take the reading glasses you swore you didn't need.

You hold it to the light —

and there, in the gold confusion,

you find the one loop

that is marginally more willing than the others.

You work with that.

You do not force. You suggest.

You find the next willing loop.

And the next.

And this is the second lesson:

every tangle has an entry point.

Every knot has one loop that is more open than the rest.

The work is finding it —

and then having the patience

to follow from there,

slow,

unhurried,

trusting that what went in

can, with enough care,

come back out.

The necklace opens.

You hold it up.

Gold and whole and worth the hour.

You put it away properly this time.
