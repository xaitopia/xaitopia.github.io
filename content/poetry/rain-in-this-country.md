The rain in this country
does not apologise for itself.
It comes suddenly and it comes with intention,
the way an elder enters a room —
everything adjusts.

You learn early:
do not spread your cloth
when there are clouds at the tree line.
Do not trust a morning that is too clear.
Do not walk without checking the sky
the way you check a stranger's hands.

My father stood in the doorway
each evening of rainy season
and I stood beside him
learning to read the light —
the particular bruised yellow
that means twenty minutes,
the stillness of birds
that means less.

He never explained it.
He just stood there
and the knowing
moved from his body into mine
the way most important things travel:
without words,
through proximity,
over years.

The rain in other countries
is a nuisance, they tell me.
Something to dress around.
Something the forecast neutralises
into inconvenience.

Here it is a negotiation.
You work with it or against it
and the ground keeps score.
The farmers know.
The motorcyclists know.
The children who run into it
bare-chested and laughing
know something the rest of us
have been educated out of knowing:

that to stand in the rain
of the country that made you
is not to get wet.
It is to be recognised.
