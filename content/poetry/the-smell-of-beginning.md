There is a smell that means everything is still possible.

It is not perfume. It is not flowers or rain or bread —

though all of those are good.

It is cedar shavings from a pencil sharpener

and the waxy, crayon warmth of a brand-new colour

still in its skin, tip still perfect,

not yet asked to be anything.

In Nigeria, we knew it:

the new exercise book, hard-covered, ruled neat,

with its smell of promise and pressed paper,

your name written in ink on the first line

by a parent who held the pen steadier than you could.

We carried those books like small altars

through compound gates and school corridors

into classrooms that smelled of chalk and ceiling fans

and the particular hope of a morning

that has not yet become an afternoon.

Everything could still happen.

You had not yet made any of the mistakes.

The page was clean.

The crayon was still sharp.

The pencil had not touched anything yet.

This is what I think of when people ask about hope —

not the grand kind, not the kind in speeches.

The small kind.

The cedar-and-wax kind.

The first day of school kind,

when everything was still ahead

and your name was written

and the book was new

and you were ready.
