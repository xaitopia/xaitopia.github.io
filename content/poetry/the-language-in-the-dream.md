In sleep I speak the language of my blood.

The words come whole, unborrowed, clean, and mine.

What waking drowned returns beneath the mud.

My grandmother spoke it like a flood —

each sentence root and river and deep vine.

In sleep I speak the language of my blood.

The schools said: English now. We understood

and lost what cannot be replaced by design.

What waking drowned returns beneath the mud.

But in the dream the mother tongue holds good,

each vowel round and rightful, a clear sign.

In sleep I speak the language of my blood.

The proverbs come. The tone marks as they should.

The self that was always Yoruba, Igbo, mine —

what waking drowned returns beneath the mud.

I wake. The words go quiet in the flood.

But I was there. I heard. I felt the line.

In sleep I speak the language of my blood.

What waking drowned returns beneath the mud.
