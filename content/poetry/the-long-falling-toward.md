They have been falling toward each other

for longer than anything alive

has been alive to watch.

 

You would not call it movement

if you were standing beside it —

only if you went away

and came back in ten million years

would you notice anything had shifted,

the way you only notice children growing

in the photographs.

 

But they are moving.

One spiral arm reaching without reaching,

two systems bending their light

toward each other

across a distance

that does not have a human name.

 

There is no collision in the way we mean it.

No impact. No shattering.

Just this:

a slow, impossible rearrangement —

stars that belonged to one darkness

finding themselves gradually

inside another.

Old constellations dissolving,

new ones forming

from the mathematics of the meeting.

 

I think about this when I think about us.

How we did not crash.

How we bent toward each other

over years — over coffees, over silences,

over all the ordinary distances —

until one morning I woke

and could no longer tell

which light was mine

and which was yours.

 

And how that was not a loss.

How that was the whole point.

How two spirals, given time enough,

don't destroy each other.

They make something

that was not possible alone.
