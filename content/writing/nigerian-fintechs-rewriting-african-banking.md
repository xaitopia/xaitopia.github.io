In 2019, a motorcycle taxi driver in Ibadan named Tunde opened his first bank account. He was 34. Not because no bank existed in his city — branches of Zenith, GTBank, and Access lined the high streets — but because none of them had been built for him. The minimum balance requirements, the documentation demands, the ATM queues that swallowed whole afternoons: the formal banking system had always functioned as a filter, keeping people like Tunde at its edges.

What changed in 2019 was OPay.

Tunde downloaded the app on a Nokia feature phone, registered with just his phone number and a selfie, and within ten minutes had a wallet, a virtual card, and access to a payment rail that connected him to every merchant, every utility, every family member who needed money sent before nightfall. "It felt like they built it for people like me," he told me when I met him at a bus park in Agodi. "Not for people in suits."

This is the shift that Nigeria's fintech generation has engineered — and it is one of the most consequential financial transformations on the African continent in the past decade.

---

## The Numbers Behind the Revolution

Nigeria's financial exclusion rate stood at roughly 36 percent in 2020, according to EFInA's Access to Financial Services survey. That means more than 38 million adults had no relationship with any formal financial institution — no bank account, no insurance, no pension. They managed money through rotating savings groups called *ajo* or *esusu*, through mattresses, through trust.

The legacy banks were not ignorant of this gap. They simply had no profitable model for closing it. The cost of maintaining physical branches, of onboarding customers who carried no salary slips or utility bills, of lending to people whose credit histories did not exist — none of it pencilled out.

Fintechs broke that calculus by rewriting the cost structure from the ground up.

Companies like **Moniepoint** (formerly TeamApt), **PalmPay**, **Kuda**, and **OPay** built on mobile infrastructure instead of brick and mortar. They used alternative data — airtime top-up frequency, mobile money transaction patterns, merchant sales volumes — to model creditworthiness where FICO scores were meaningless. They turned small-scale traders into agents, creating a last-mile distribution network that no branch rollout could have matched in speed or coverage.

By 2023, Moniepoint alone was processing more than $12 billion in monthly transactions and had been named by the Financial Times as one of Africa's fastest-growing companies. These are not small experiments. They are systemic replacements.

## The Product Logic That Banks Missed

To understand why fintechs succeeded where banks stalled, you have to understand how money actually moves in the Nigerian informal economy.

Most transactions happen in person, in cash, often between people who know each other. A market woman in Onitsha buys tomatoes on credit from a supplier she has done business with for fifteen years. A suya seller in Abuja receives daily sales in coins and hundreds. A seamstress in Lagos collects advance payments for dresses that do not yet exist.

The financial products these people needed were not mortgages or mutual funds. They needed:

- **Instant, fee-free transfers** between people who know each other
- **Small, short-term credit** tied to the rhythm of their working week
- **Easy bill payments** without standing in queues
- **A way to save** without a minimum balance hanging over their heads

Kuda Bank, which launched in 2019 as Nigeria's first fully digital bank, built every feature around these needs. No maintenance fees. No transfer charges within the app. A "spend + save" feature that rounds up every transaction and sets the change aside. Overdrafts of up to ₦20,000 for users who qualify based on usage patterns — not paperwork.

It sounds simple. It is simple. That was the point.

## The Regulatory Tightrope

Nigeria's Central Bank has not been a passive observer of this transformation. The CBN's approach has been what analysts call "guided disruption" — aggressive licensing regimes that kept unserious players out while giving serious ones enough runway to experiment.

The Payment Service Bank (PSB) licence, introduced in 2018, was a masterstroke: it allowed telecoms companies and fintech operators to offer savings accounts and payment services without becoming full commercial banks, lowering the capital threshold dramatically. MTN's MoMo, Airtel Money, and others moved quickly.

But the CBN has also shown its teeth. In 2021, it barred commercial banks from servicing cryptocurrency exchanges — a decision that pushed crypto activity underground and onto peer-to-peer platforms rather than eliminating it. The 2023 naira redesign crisis, in which the physical cash supply collapsed while digital alternatives lagged, briefly illuminated how thin the infrastructure still is in rural areas when data connectivity fails.

The fintechs that will dominate the next decade are those building not just for the smartphone-in-Lagos customer, but for the feature phone user in Damaturu — and building networks robust enough to function when the grid does not cooperate.

---

## What Comes Next

The next frontier is credit.

Nigeria's consumer credit market is still embryonic by global standards. Household debt as a percentage of GDP sits below 5 percent — compared to over 75 percent in South Africa and more than 100 percent in the United States. This is not because Nigerians do not want credit. It is because the risk models for extending it have never worked at scale.

Fintechs like **Carbon** (formerly Paylater), **FairMoney**, and **Renmoney** are building those models now — using transaction data, behavioral signals, and machine learning to price risk for people who have never had a formal credit history. The default rates are not always pretty. But the iteration speed is unlike anything the banking sector has ever managed.

There is also the question of cross-border money movement. With over 1.7 million Nigerians in the diaspora sending roughly $20 billion home each year, the remittance corridor is one of the most active financial highways in the developing world. Startups like **Grey**, **Leatherback**, and **Nala** are attacking the fee structures of Western Union and MoneyGram with the same logic that Kuda applied to savings accounts: build lean, price honestly, and earn loyalty.

---

Tunde, the motorcycle driver in Ibadan, now uses OPay to collect fares, pay his fuel supplier, and send money to his mother in Kwara State. He has taken two small business loans — ₦15,000 each — to cover costs during slow weeks, and repaid both on time. He is building a credit history, brick by brick, without ever walking into a bank.

"I don't even know where the nearest bank branch is," he told me. "I don't need to."

That sentence, more than any investor deck or policy paper, captures what Nigerian fintechs have actually achieved.
