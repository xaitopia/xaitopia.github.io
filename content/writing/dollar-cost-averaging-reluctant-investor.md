Most people who have not started investing will tell you they are waiting for the right time.

They are watching the market. When inflation comes down, they will start. When the election is over, they will start. When the dollar stabilises, when rates drop, when things settle, when they have more certainty — they will start.

The uncomfortable truth is that this waiting is itself a financial decision, and it is rarely the right one. The strategy that releases you from the obligation of knowing when to enter the market is also one of the most rigorously tested in the history of investing. It is called **dollar-cost averaging**, and its entire logic rests on a counterintuitive idea: you do not need to be right about timing. You just need to be consistent.

---

## What Dollar-Cost Averaging Actually Is

Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is the practice of investing a fixed amount of money at regular intervals — weekly, monthly, quarterly — regardless of what the market is doing at the time of each purchase.

If you invest ₦20,000 in an index fund every month, you will buy more units when the price is low and fewer units when the price is high. Over time, your average cost per unit tends to be lower than if you had tried to pick the "perfect" entry point — and almost certainly lower than if you had waited indefinitely for certainty that never comes.

A simple example:

| Month | Investment | Unit Price | Units Purchased |
|-------|-----------|------------|-----------------|
| January | ₦20,000 | ₦200 | 100 |
| February | ₦20,000 | ₦160 | 125 |
| March | ₦20,000 | ₦250 | 80 |
| April | ₦20,000 | ₦180 | 111 |

Total invested: ₦80,000. Total units: 416. Average cost per unit: ₦192.30.

If you had invested all ₦80,000 in January at ₦200 per unit, you would own 400 units. The DCA approach, by buying more in the dips, produced 16 extra units without any prediction, any market-timing, any skill.

This is not magic. It is arithmetic applied to the reality that markets fluctuate and you cannot reliably predict which months will be cheap. So you buy every month and let the maths do its work.

---

## Why It Works Psychologically, Not Just Mathematically

Investing is not just a numbers problem. It is a behaviour problem.

The biggest threat to any long-term investment strategy is the investor abandoning it at the worst possible moment. Markets fall. Portfolios turn red. The newspaper headlines become alarming. And people sell — locking in losses they would have recovered in three years, turning paper declines into permanent ones.

DCA helps here not because it eliminates volatility (it does not), but because it reframes how you experience it.

When you have invested a lump sum and the market drops 25 percent, that is an emergency. Your ₦1,000,000 is now worth ₦750,000 and everything in you says to do something.

When you are on a DCA schedule and the market drops 25 percent, that is a sale. Your next monthly contribution will buy 33 percent more units than last month. The drop is working *for* you, not against you.

This psychological flip — from "the market is falling" to "the market is cheaper" — is one of the most valuable mental shifts a long-term investor can make. DCA installs it automatically.

## The Case Against DCA (and Why It Doesn't Matter for Most People)

Mathematically speaking, DCA is not the optimal strategy if you already have a lump sum available to invest.

Several studies — including research by Vanguard — have found that in markets that trend upward over time, investing a lump sum immediately outperforms spreading it over twelve months approximately two-thirds of the time. The logic is simple: if markets tend to go up, you want to be invested as early as possible.

This is true. It is also largely irrelevant to the situation most people are actually in.

Most people do not have a large lump sum sitting idle. They have an income that arrives monthly and expenses that consume most of it. The realistic choice is not "invest ₦2,400,000 today versus ₦200,000 per month for twelve months." It is "invest ₦200,000 per month versus spend it or let it accumulate in a savings account."

For people building investment habits from current income — which is the majority of people who are not yet investing — DCA is not a compromise. It is the appropriate strategy for the way they actually earn and live.

## How to Start in Nigeria

A DCA strategy requires three things: a regular income, a recurring transfer mechanism, and an investment vehicle that accepts regular contributions.

**For stocks and ETFs:** Platforms like **Bamboo**, **Chaka**, and **Trove** allow Nigerians to invest in US-listed ETFs (including S&P 500 index funds like VOO or SPY) with relatively small minimum amounts. Bamboo and Trove allow auto-invest features or easy recurring manual transfers. Dollar funding goes through their wallet systems at prevailing exchange rates.

**For Nigerian equities:** The **Nigerian Exchange (NGX)** is accessible through stockbrokers and, increasingly, through apps like **Meristem**, **Stanbic IBTC**, and **ARM Securities**. For index-style exposure to the Nigerian market, the **NewGold ETF** or broadly diversified mutual funds through Stanbic or Chapel Hill Denham provide diversification without stock-picking.

**For a simpler entry:** If the platforms above feel like too much setup, a money market fund with a standing monthly transfer is a reasonable starting point. It will not give you equity-like returns over time, but it builds the *habit* of regular investing — which matters enormously.

The amount matters less than the consistency. ₦10,000 per month, every month, for five years, is worth more than ₦200,000 invested once and never revisited.

---

## The One Rule

If you are going to try DCA, there is one rule that supersedes all others: **do not stop during a downturn.**

The months when the market is falling and your portfolio is red and everything feels wrong — those are the months when DCA does its most important work. Those are the months when you are buying more units at lower prices. Stopping then is the equivalent of quitting a gym membership in February because the scale has not moved yet.

The strategy only works if you stay in it. The entry point is nearly irrelevant. The exit point matters enormously. The years of consistent contribution in between are everything.

You do not need to know when the market will rise. You just need to keep showing up.
