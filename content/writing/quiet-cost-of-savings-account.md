There is a particular kind of financial comfort that comes from watching a savings balance grow slowly, month by month, in the same bank account you have held since university. The number goes up. Nothing bad happens. This feels, understandably, like prudence.

It is not prudence. In a high-inflation economy, it is a slow leak — and most people never notice until the water is already on the floor.

---

## The Maths Nobody Shows You

Let's run the numbers honestly.

Suppose you have ₦500,000 sitting in a standard savings account at a commercial bank in Nigeria. The average savings interest rate at major banks hovers around 1.25 percent per annum. After one year, your balance reads ₦506,250. You have earned ₦6,250.

Now suppose inflation over that same year ran at 28 percent — which, as of mid-2024, was Nigeria's approximate headline inflation rate according to the National Bureau of Statistics.

The ₦500,000 you deposited could buy, say, a generator, a decent laptop, three months of groceries, or any number of things at last year's prices. At the end of the year, those same things cost approximately ₦640,000. Your ₦506,250 cannot buy them anymore. You have gained ₦6,250 in nominal terms and lost roughly ₦133,750 in real terms.

Your money grew. Your purchasing power shrank. This is what economists call a **negative real interest rate**, and it is the silent tax that inflation levies on people who do nothing.

---

## Why We Keep Doing It Anyway

The reasons people park money in savings accounts despite this erosion are mostly psychological, and they are not stupid reasons.

**Safety.** A savings account at a NDIC-insured bank carries a guarantee (up to ₦5 million per depositor) that no investment can honestly offer. When the instinct to preserve capital is strong — after a financial shock, a job loss, a market crash — the guaranteed return of your principal matters more than its real value.

**Liquidity.** You can access a savings account at any ATM at midnight on a Sunday. A Treasury Bill, a mutual fund unit, or a fixed deposit locks your money up in ways that create genuine inconvenience. For people living close to the edge of their income, liquidity is not a luxury — it is a survival tool.

**Familiarity.** The interface, the branch, the SMS alerts — you have used this system for years. The activation energy required to open a money market account or buy a T-bill feels disproportionate to a gain you cannot see in real time.

These are rational constraints. The problem arises when people with *neither* a liquidity crisis *nor* a risk crisis *nor* a familiarity barrier keep money idle in savings accounts by default — not by decision.

---

## What the Alternatives Actually Look Like

You do not need to become a sophisticated investor to stop losing money to inflation. The gap between a savings account and slightly better options is smaller than most people imagine.

**Treasury Bills.** The Central Bank of Nigeria auctions 91-day, 182-day, and 364-day T-bills regularly. As of late 2024, stop rates on the 364-day bill were hovering around 18–22 percent depending on the auction. This is still below headline inflation, but it is dramatically better than 1.25 percent. T-bills are backed by the Federal Government of Nigeria, carry virtually no credit risk, and can be purchased through most commercial banks or online brokers like Stanbic IBTC, Chapel Hill Denham, or ARM.

**Money Market Funds.** Products like the ARM Money Market Fund, Stanbic IBTC Money Market Fund, or United Capital Money Market Fund invest in short-term government and corporate debt instruments. They are not capital-guaranteed in the way a bank deposit is, but they have historically maintained stable net asset values. Yields have ranged from 15 to 22 percent in recent periods. Minimum investments start as low as ₦5,000. Redemption is typically within one to two business days.

**Fixed Deposits.** Most banks offer fixed deposit rates significantly higher than their savings rates — often 8 to 14 percent for 90-to-180-day tenors. Not great, but better. If your objection to moving money is the friction of learning a new platform, a fixed deposit at your existing bank is the path of least resistance.

**Dollar-denominated savings.** For people who earn or receive funds in naira but worry about sustained currency depreciation, keeping a portion of savings in a domiciliary account or through platforms like Grey or Leatherback can serve as a partial hedge. This introduces FX risk in the other direction — dollar holdings lose value in naira terms if the naira strengthens — so it is not a blanket recommendation.

---

## The Emergency Fund Exception

One rule matters above all others: **do not optimise your emergency fund.**

Standard personal finance guidance suggests holding three to six months of living expenses in cash or near-cash, accessible immediately. This money should be in a savings account or money market fund with same-day or next-day liquidity. Do not lock it into a 364-day T-bill to chase an extra 8 percent. The point of an emergency fund is not return — it is readiness. The cost of not having it when you need it will always exceed whatever you gain by chasing yield.

Beyond your emergency fund, however, the calculus changes. Every naira beyond that buffer that sits in a 1.25 percent savings account is paying an opportunity cost that compounds quietly, year after year.

---

## A Practical Starting Point

You do not need to move everything. A useful first step: identify any money in your savings account that you have not touched in three months and do not expect to need in the next three months. That money has revealed itself as non-emergency. It has an alternative use.

Open a money market fund account — most take less than fifteen minutes online — and move that amount. Check back in six months. The habit of asking "is this money working hard enough?" is more valuable than any specific instrument you put it into.

The savings account is not the enemy. Leaving money there *without thinking about it* is.
